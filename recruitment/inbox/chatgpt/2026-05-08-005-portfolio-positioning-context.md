## Seniority signal

For Staff / Principal Product Designer roles, the portfolio needs to prove more than polished screens. It needs to show senior-level judgment under ambiguity.

The strongest Staff / Principal signals to emphasize:
- Decision quality: why Eddie chose one product/design path over another, not just what the final UI looked like.
- Strategic judgment: how he framed the problem, identified leverage, sequenced work, and connected design decisions to business or customer outcomes.
- Product/system complexity: how he handled search systems, marketplace dynamics, taxonomy, AI behavior, cross-platform patterns, or multi-stakeholder constraints.
- Cross-functional influence: how he shaped direction with product, engineering, data, leadership, research, and other designers.
- Ambiguity handling: how he moved work forward when the problem, evidence, technology, or organizational alignment was incomplete.
- Evidence discipline: what was known, what was measured, what was qualitative, what was inferred, and what should not be overstated.
- Tradeoff clarity: what was sacrificed, simplified, postponed, generalized, or de-risked.
- Ability to raise design quality beyond one surface: standards, patterns, frameworks, review rituals, enablement, platform thinking, and reusable product principles.
- Ownership clarity: where Eddie personally led, decided, influenced, facilitated, or supported.
- Outcome maturity: impact should be included only when safe, verified, and properly attributed.

The portfolio should avoid relying on:
- generic double-diamond process steps
- vague strategy language
- screenshots without context
- inflated “I drove X% growth” claims without evidence and attribution
- AI buzzwords that do not connect to product decisions
- over-explaining methods instead of explaining judgment

## Portfolio review narrative

Use this reusable structure for portfolio reviews. Adapt the depth depending on the interview audience and time limit.

### 1. Current positioning

Open with the present-day positioning:

“I’m a Principal Product Designer and AI Design Lead focused on marketplace/search systems, cross-platform buyer experiences, and practical AI product strategy. My portfolio is built around work where the design challenge was not just interface quality, but product judgment, system complexity, and organizational alignment.”

Keep it short. Do not start with a long career history.

### 2. Why this case matters

Explain why the case is strategically relevant:
- It affected a high-value product surface.
- It involved complex marketplace/search behavior.
- It required cross-platform coherence.
- It created reusable standards or product-system improvements.
- It shows how Eddie works through ambiguity.
- It connects directly to the company’s product challenges.

Avoid:
- “I wanted to improve the user experience.”
- “This was a design thinking process.”
- “We redesigned the UI to make it cleaner.”

### 3. Product/problem context

Give enough context for the audience to understand the system:
- Who the user/customer is
- What they are trying to do
- Why the product surface matters
- What business or product tension existed
- What constraints shaped the work

Keep this concise. The goal is orientation, not a lecture.

### 4. What was hard

Name the actual difficulty:
- Conflicting customer and business needs
- Search/listing complexity
- Marketplace trust and decision confidence
- Platform differences
- Measurement ambiguity
- Internal alignment
- Technical constraints
- AI behavior and safety constraints
- Lack of clear precedent
- Rollout risk

This section is often where seniority becomes visible.

### 5. Key decisions and tradeoffs

Show the judgment:
- What Eddie changed
- What Eddie chose not to change
- What tradeoffs were made
- How risks were reduced
- How the team balanced short-term delivery with long-term system quality
- What evidence influenced the direction
- Where Eddie had to influence or align stakeholders

Make the choices explicit. Do not hide behind process language.

### 6. Outcome and evidence, only if safe and verified

Use outcomes only when they are:
- verified in the repo
- safe to discuss
- correctly attributed
- not exposing internal dashboards, experiment data, roadmap details, or confidential source evidence

If metrics are not safe or not verified, use safer framing:
- “The work was validated through [general method], but I keep the internal details out of the public portfolio.”
- “I can discuss the decision-making and tradeoffs, but I’m careful not to disclose internal experiment data.”
- “The published case study uses generalized outcome framing because the underlying evidence is internal.”

Do not invent metrics. Do not round or paraphrase internal metrics unless the repo explicitly supports it.

### 7. What this shows about seniority

Translate the case into hiring signal:
- “This shows how I handle product-system complexity.”
- “This shows how I move from ambiguous strategy to usable product direction.”
- “This shows how I influence across product, engineering, data, and design.”
- “This shows how I use evidence without overstating attribution.”
- “This shows how I improve design quality beyond one surface.”

This section should connect directly to Staff / Principal expectations.

### 8. What Eddie would do differently or next

End with maturity:
- What could be improved
- What was constrained
- What Eddie would measure next
- What he would systematize
- What he would revisit with better data, better tooling, or a stronger operating model

This should sound like a senior operator, not a defensive explanation.

## Confidentiality and claim-safety reminders

AutoScout24 work needs strict confidentiality and evidence discipline.

Rules:
- Do not assume launched work means internal evidence is safe to publish.
- Do not publish internal dashboards, raw experiment results, Jira details, stakeholder decks, roadmap artifacts, strategy documents, or internal source evidence.
- Do not expose unreleased AI concepts, internal AI roadmaps, sensitive prototypes, internal standards, or proprietary workflows.
- Do not show internal benchmark data unless the repo explicitly marks it as public-safe or properly generalized.
- Do not copy internal language into public case studies.
- Do not imply company endorsement of Eddie’s portfolio claims.
- Do not present confidential material as “safe” just because it appears in a live product.
- Do not use exact metrics unless they are verified in the repo and cleared for the intended audience.
- Do not combine public screenshots with private internal reasoning in a way that reveals confidential strategy.
- Do not expose stakeholder names, internal team structures, private decision logs, or source document titles if they are sensitive.
- Do not use AI prototypes or concept names publicly unless they are verified as safe.

Safer patterns:
- Generalize internal evidence.
- Describe decision logic without exposing source material.
- Use outcome framing only when verified and attributable.
- Say “internal evidence” rather than showing the evidence.
- Use anonymized or recreated artifacts where needed.
- Mark uncertain claims as “verify in repo.”
- Keep sensitive detail for live interview discussion only when appropriate and still confidentiality-safe.

Metrics rule:
- Exact metrics belong only where the repo supports them.
- Metrics need source, scope, date, platform, attribution, and safety status.
- If any of those are missing, mark the metric as “verify in repo” or remove it from public-facing material.

## Writing and tone guidance

Portfolio writing should be:
- direct
- mature
- specific
- concise
- evidence-aware
- senior without inflation
- practical
- human
- confident but not performative
- clear enough for recruiters and credible enough for senior design leaders

Avoid:
- corporate filler
- generic AI language
- process theatre
- vague strategy claims
- inflated impact statements
- “passionate about” language
- “leveraged design thinking” phrasing
- long method explanations with no decision quality
- buzzwords that do not connect to evidence
- claims that sound bigger than the proof

Preferred writing pattern:
- Start with the product or business problem.
- Explain why it mattered.
- Show the hard constraint or tradeoff.
- State the key decision.
- Connect to outcome or learning only when verified.
- Explain what this proves about Eddie’s seniority.

Ownership language:
- Use “I” for direct ownership and decisions.
- Use “we” for genuinely shared work.
- Do not overstate individual attribution.
- Do not hide Eddie’s leadership behind “the team” when he personally led a decision.
- Do not claim sole ownership of shared product outcomes.
- Separate “I led,” “I designed,” “I partnered,” “I influenced,” “I facilitated,” and “the team shipped.”

AI writing guidance:
- Avoid generic “AI transformation” language.
- Anchor AI claims in concrete product behavior, user needs, design standards, prototypes, tooling workflows, and organizational enablement.
- Show how AI changed the design problem, not just that AI was involved.
- Make clear whether work was shipped, prototyped, strategic, or internal enablement.
