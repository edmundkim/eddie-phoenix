## Application / cover letter fragments

Use these as building blocks. Do not paste all of them into one application.

### Why AI product design

AI product design is interesting to me because the interaction model is still being defined. The work is not just about making complex technology look simple. It is about deciding what the product should do, when it should ask for input, how much control users need, what trust looks like, and how teams make powerful systems feel useful instead of unpredictable.

### Why ambiguous product categories

I am drawn to product categories where the patterns are not settled yet. That is where design has real leverage: framing the problem, reducing ambiguity, testing interaction models, and helping teams decide what the product should become before the market has a fixed answer.

### Why Staff / Principal IC scope

I am looking for Staff / Principal IC scope because my best work happens when I can stay close to the product while also shaping direction across teams. I want enough ambiguity and responsibility to influence strategy, but enough hands-on design work to keep the ideas grounded.

### Why marketplace/search/system complexity matters

Marketplace and search products force design to deal with real complexity: user intent, inventory structure, ranking, filtering, comparison, trust, conversion, and cross-platform consistency. I like that kind of work because small design decisions can change how people understand options and make decisions.

### Why Eddie’s background is relevant

My background combines marketplace product design, cross-platform buyer experience, AI design leadership, and senior IC strategy. I can work across discovery, product direction, systems, prototyping, and delivery, while staying careful about evidence, tradeoffs, and what can actually ship.

### AI-native product fragment

I am especially interested in AI-native products where design is part of defining the product behavior itself. The valuable design work is not only interface polish. It is shaping collaboration between users and intelligent systems: what the system does, what the user controls, how feedback works, and how the product earns trust over repeated use.

### Developer tooling / technical UX fragment

I am interested in technical product environments where the user has high intent, high context, and low patience for vague product thinking. In those spaces, design has to be precise: clear mental models, strong defaults, fast feedback, and careful tradeoffs between power and usability.

## Recruiter screen talking points

### “Tell me about yourself”

I am a Principal Product Designer and AI Design Lead based in Berlin. My recent work has focused on buyer experience at AutoScout24, especially search, browse, listing experiences, and cross-platform product systems. I also lead AI design work: helping translate AI capabilities into usable product patterns, standards, and prototypes.

The through-line in my career is designing complex product systems where users need to make decisions with confidence. Right now, I am exploring Staff / Principal IC roles where I can combine product strategy, hands-on design, AI interaction work, and senior cross-functional leadership.

### “What are you looking for next?”

I am looking for a Staff or Principal IC role with real product scope. Ideally, the role sits close to AI, marketplace/search, platform, developer tooling, or complex product systems. I want to work somewhere with a high product bar, strong technical ambition, and enough ambiguity that design can shape direction rather than only execute requirements.

### “Why are you leaving / exploring?”

I am not leaving from a place of urgency. I have strong scope and trust in my current role, but I am at the point where I want to test whether there is a stronger environment for the next stage of my career. I am especially interested in companies where AI, product ambition, and senior IC design scope are more central to the company’s trajectory.

### “Why this role?”

This role stood out because it seems to combine [product area], [level of ambiguity], and [scope]. That matches the work I do best: taking complex product spaces, making the user and business problem clearer, shaping practical product direction, and staying close enough to the design details to make sure the experience actually works.

### “What kind of scope are you looking for?”

I am looking for broad IC scope, not a narrow feature-design role. I want to shape direction, influence product strategy, align with PM and engineering, define patterns or systems where needed, and still produce strong hands-on design work. I am comfortable with ambiguity, but I want the role to have enough authority and access to make good decisions possible.

### “How do you work with PM/engineering/research?”

I try to work as a product partner, not as a service function. With PMs, I care about problem framing, tradeoffs, sequencing, and how we connect design decisions to product outcomes. With engineers, I try to understand constraints early, prototype where useful, and make decisions that are buildable. With research, I want the insights to sharpen product judgment, not become process theatre.

### “How do you think about AI design?”

I think good AI design starts with product behavior, not interface decoration. The key questions are: what job should the AI do, what should stay under user control, how does the system explain itself, how does the user recover from mistakes, and how does trust build over time? The best AI products make powerful capability feel understandable, useful, and safe enough to rely on.

## Portfolio review framing

The portfolio repo is the source of truth for case study content. Do not reconstruct case studies from this extraction. Use this section only as a reusable presentation and framing guide.

### Recommended presentation flow

1. **Opening: who I am and what I want to show**
   - 60 to 90 seconds.
   - Frame the through-line: AI design leadership, marketplace/search systems, cross-platform product design, product systems, and senior IC judgment.

2. **Case study 1: strongest evidence for product impact**
   - Use the most credible shipped work.
   - Focus on problem, stakes, decisions, tradeoffs, and outcome.
   - Do not over-explain the design process.

3. **Case study 2: strongest evidence for seniority / ambiguity**
   - Use work that shows strategy, systems thinking, cross-functional influence, or AI leadership.
   - Make clear what Eddie personally drove versus what the team owned.

4. **Optional supporting example**
   - Use only if it strengthens the specific role fit.
   - Keep it short: one problem, one decision, one outcome or learning.

5. **Close by connecting to the role**
   - State why these examples are relevant to the company’s product problems.
   - Avoid a generic “I am excited” ending.

### Case framing pattern

For each case, use this order:

1. **Problem**
   - What was broken, unclear, inefficient, risky, or strategically important?

2. **Stakes**
   - Why did it matter for users, the business, or the product system?

3. **Role**
   - What did Eddie personally own, lead, influence, or decide?

4. **Constraints**
   - What made the problem hard: technical, organizational, platform, data, trust, marketplace, timing, or cross-team complexity?

5. **Key decisions**
   - What were the 2 to 4 important product/design decisions?

6. **Tradeoffs**
   - What was intentionally not done, simplified, delayed, or compromised?

7. **Outcome**
   - Use public-safe, evidence-backed outcomes only.
   - If attribution is partial, say so.

8. **Learning / relevance**
   - What does this prove about Eddie’s judgment for the target role?

### How to show seniority without process theatre

Show seniority through:
- problem framing
- prioritization
- tradeoffs
- stakeholder alignment
- product judgment
- evidence discipline
- systems thinking
- cross-platform implications
- ability to connect strategy to delivery

Avoid seniority theatre:
- long process diagrams
- generic double-diamond narration
- inflated strategy claims
- “I led” without evidence
- screenshots without decision context
- internal artifacts that cannot be explained publicly

### How to protect confidential details

Use public-safe framing:
- describe the type of evidence without showing confidential source material
- use directional or generalized outcomes only when exact metrics are not approved for public use
- avoid internal dashboards, unreleased roadmap details, stakeholder documents, experiment names, internal benchmarks, and sensitive screenshots
- remove company-specific strategy language that reveals internal plans
- separate public portfolio claims from private interview-only notes

If a claim depends on internal evidence that cannot be shown, either generalize it or say it only in a live conversation with careful framing.

### How to connect portfolio examples to AI/product/company roles

Use bridge statements like:

- “The relevant part of this case for your team is not the automotive context. It is the system problem: helping users make high-confidence decisions in a complex product environment.”
- “This case is useful for AI roles because it shows how I think about trust, control, progressive disclosure, and product behavior when the system has complexity behind the interface.”
- “This example shows the kind of Staff / Principal scope I am looking for: ambiguous problem, cross-functional constraints, product direction, and hands-on design decisions.”
- “The marketplace context matters because it forced design decisions to connect user intent, business outcomes, and platform consistency.”

## Follow-up questions for recruiters / hiring managers

Use 3 to 5 questions per conversation. Do not overload the interviewer.

### Role scope

- What are the most important problems this person would own in the first 6 to 12 months?
- Is this role expected to define product direction, execute on an existing roadmap, or both?
- Where does this role sit in the team: embedded in one product area, across multiple teams, or as a horizontal design lead?
- What would make someone successful at Staff / Principal level here?

### Team maturity

- How mature is the design function today, and where does it need to level up?
- What are the biggest gaps in the current product/design process?
- How do design, product, engineering, and research typically work together?
- Is the team looking for someone to bring structure, craft, strategic direction, or all three?

### Success criteria

- What would a strong first 90 days look like?
- What would success look like after one year?
- How will the role be evaluated: shipped product impact, team influence, quality of thinking, design craft, business outcomes, or something else?
- What would cause someone to struggle in this role?

### Design/product decision-making

- How are major product decisions made today?
- Where does design have the most influence, and where is the team still trying to improve that influence?
- How often are designers involved before a solution direction is already chosen?
- How does the team balance speed, craft, and evidence?

### Pace and work-life balance

- How would you describe the pace and operating style of the team? I’m comfortable with high standards and intense product work, but I try to understand whether a company’s pace is sustainable or more consistently high-pressure.
- Are there specific periods where the team runs especially intensely, or is that the normal operating mode?
- How does the company handle urgency without burning people out?
- What does “high performance” look like day to day here?

### Hands-on versus leadership expectations

- How much of the role is expected to be hands-on product design versus direction-setting and alignment?
- Does the team need someone to raise craft quality, shape strategy, mentor others, or directly drive a product area?
- Is this role more of a principal IC role, a design lead role, or a step toward management?
- How much time would this person spend working in Figma / prototypes versus meetings and strategy?

### AI/product strategy

- Where is AI core to the product today, and where is it still exploratory?
- What are the hardest AI product design questions the team is facing?
- How does the team think about trust, control, user feedback, and failure states in AI experiences?
- Is the design challenge more about inventing new AI-native workflows or integrating AI into an existing product system?

### Relocation / hybrid expectations, high level only

- How important is location for this role?
- Is hybrid presence required for product collaboration, team culture, or company policy?
- If relocation is expected, what timeline does the company usually support?
- Is there flexibility for a transition period if the role and fit are strong?

## Compensation / location language

Only reusable, non-private wording is included here. Do not add current salary, minimum acceptable salary, detailed negotiation strategy, or private visa details.

### Broad compensation expectation language

I am evaluating roles based on scope, company trajectory, product fit, and total package. For the right Staff / Principal IC role, I would want compensation to reflect the level of responsibility, market expectations, and the impact expected from the role.

### If asked for expectations early

I would prefer to understand the scope and level calibration first, because compensation should match whether this is truly Staff / Principal scope. Broadly, I am looking for a competitive total package for a senior IC product design role, including base, bonus if applicable, and equity where relevant.

### If the company asks for flexibility

I can be flexible depending on the role’s scope, company trajectory, equity upside, and location requirements. The important thing for me is that the package matches the level of ownership and the opportunity cost of making a move.

### Relocation tradeoff language

I am open to relocation for the right opportunity, but I would weigh it against the role’s scope, company trajectory, product fit, and total package. A relocation-heavy move needs to be clearly stronger than staying in Berlin.

### Hybrid / location language

I am comfortable with hybrid work when it supports collaboration and product quality. I would want to understand whether office presence is about meaningful team work or simply policy.

## Tone rules

Use this tone for recruiter messages, applications, follow-ups, and interview prep:

- concise
- confident
- specific
- human
- senior without sounding inflated
- direct about fit
- evidence-aware
- not desperate
- not over-explaining
- not fake-polished
- not generic
- not overly formal
- no corporate filler
- no “I am passionate about AI” language
- no vague “driving innovation” language
- no exaggerated ownership claims
- no unsupported impact claims
- no long autobiographical setup
- no apologetic wording after rejection
- no over-personal details in recruiter communication

Good tone pattern:
- “This role stood out because…”
- “My background is strongest in…”
- “The relevant overlap is…”
- “I would be glad to discuss whether the scope matches what your team needs.”

Avoid:
- “I have always been passionate about…”
- “I believe my unique background makes me the perfect candidate…”
- “I am reaching out to humbly ask…”
- “I drove transformational impact…”
- “I would be grateful for any opportunity…”

## Items to keep out of materials.md

### Move to `recruitment/private/`

- current salary
- minimum acceptable compensation
- negotiation floor or walk-away numbers
- detailed visa, Blue Card, permanent residency, or immigration notes
- private emotional reflections about rejection, anxiety, urgency, or personal risk
- sensitive personal constraints that should not be in reusable recruiter material
- private company-laptop/tooling concerns if not relevant to public materials

### Move to `recruitment/companies/`

- company-specific role descriptions
- company facts, funding, valuation, ARR, headcount, office policy, or product claims
- company prioritisation notes
- P0/P1/P2/P3 rankings
- recruiter names
- hiring team names
- company-specific interview feedback
- company-specific follow-up history
- rejection details unless they create reusable messaging
- current role availability or location constraints

### Keep in portfolio repo files

- detailed case study content
- case study facts, timelines, assets, and source analyses
- AutoScout24-specific evidence
- portfolio-safe metrics and claim validation
- screenshot decisions
- confidentiality review notes
- page/component implementation details
- case study narrative drafts
- visual hierarchy and site content decisions

### Do not extract into materials.md

- detailed application tracker rows
- company prioritisation rankings
- recruiter names
- private compensation details
- visa / Blue Card details
- personal emotional reflections
- detailed rejection analysis
- detailed portfolio case study content
- AutoScout24 internal metrics
- confidential portfolio evidence
- internal documents, unreleased strategy, roadmap details, dashboards, benchmarks, or stakeholder materials
