# Materials And Messaging Guide

This is the reusable messaging layer for applications, recruiter outreach, interview prep, portfolio reviews, and role-fit conversations. It is distilled from `inbox/chatgpt/2026-05-08-003-materials-and-messaging.md`.

Use these as building blocks, not final copy. Every message should still be adapted to the company, role level, product surface, and current job description.

## Source Of Truth Rules

- Use `portfolio-site/RESUME.md`, `portfolio-site/components/PROFILE.md`, and live portfolio case-study pages for career claims.
- Use `recruitment/applications.md` for status and next actions.
- Use `recruitment/company-prioritisation.md` for company tiering and role-fit logic.

- Keep current salary, compensation floor, negotiation strategy, visa details, private emotional reflections, and sensitive personal constraints in `recruitment/private/`.
- Do not reconstruct case-study details, AutoScout24 internal evidence, confidential metrics, dashboards, roadmap details, or stakeholder material from ChatGPT memory.

Use direct ownership language for AI-assisted or conversational product experiences only when a specific case supports it.

I have worked on experiences where users need to filter complex inventory, compare options, trust the results, and take meaningful action across web and native apps. ### Staff / Principal Scope

At Staff / Principal level, I am looking for broad IC scope: ambiguous product problems, design direction, cross-functional alignment, product strategy, and enough hands-on work to keep the output sharp. I want to shape direction, make hard product calls, and help teams ship better product.

## Application Fragments

Use one or two fragments at a time. Do not stack all of them into a cover letter.

### Why AI Product Design

AI product design interests me because the interaction model is still being defined. The work asks direct product questions: what the system should do, when it should ask for input, how much control users need, how people recover from mistakes, and how trust builds over time.

### Why Ambiguous Product Categories

I am drawn to product categories where the patterns are not settled yet. That is where design has real leverage: framing the problem, reducing ambiguity, testing interaction models, and helping teams decide what the product should become before the market has a fixed answer.

### Why Staff / Principal IC Scope

I am looking for Staff / Principal IC scope because my best work happens when I can stay close to the product while also shaping direction across teams. I want enough ambiguity and responsibility to influence strategy, but enough hands-on design work to keep the ideas grounded.

### Why Marketplace/Search Complexity Matters

Marketplace and search products force design to deal with real complexity: user intent, inventory structure, ranking, filtering, comparison, trust, conversion, and cross-platform consistency. I like that kind of work because small design decisions can change how people understand options and make decisions.

### Why Eddie's Background Is Relevant

My background combines marketplace product design, cross-platform buyer experience, AI design leadership, and senior IC strategy. I can work across discovery, product direction, systems, prototyping, and delivery, while staying careful about evidence, tradeoffs, and what can actually ship.

### AI-Native Product

I am especially interested in AI-native products where design helps define the product behavior itself: what the system does, what the user controls, how feedback works, and how the product earns trust over repeated use.

### Developer Tooling / Technical UX

I am interested in technical product environments where the user has high intent, high context, and low patience for vague product thinking. In those spaces, design has to be precise: clear mental models, strong defaults, fast feedback, and careful tradeoffs between power and usability.

I want to work somewhere with a high product bar, strong technical ambition, and enough ambiguity that design can shape direction rather than only execute requirements.

### Why This Role?

That matches the work I do best: taking complex product spaces, making the user and business problem clearer, shaping practical product direction, and staying close enough to the design details to make sure the experience actually works.

### What Kind Of Scope Are You Looking For?

I am looking for broad IC scope, not a narrow feature-design role. I want to shape direction, influence product strategy, align with PM and engineering, define patterns or systems where needed, and still produce strong hands-on design work. I am comfortable with ambiguity, but I want the role to have enough authority and access to make good decisions possible.

### How Do You Work With PM, Engineering, And Research?

I try to work as a product partner, not as a service function. With PMs, I care about problem framing, tradeoffs, sequencing, and how we connect design decisions to product outcomes. With engineers, I try to understand constraints early, prototype where useful, and make decisions that are buildable. With research, I want the insights to sharpen product judgment, not become process theatre.

### Why Are You Leaving / Exploring?

I am not leaving from a place of urgency. I have strong scope and trust in my current role, but I am at the point where I want to test whether there is a stronger environment for the next stage of my career. I am especially interested in companies where AI, product ambition, and senior IC design scope are more central to the company's trajectory.

### How Do You Think About AI Design?

Good AI design starts with product behavior. The key questions are: what job should the AI do, what should stay under user control, how does the system explain itself, how does the user recover from mistakes, and how does trust build over time? Strong AI products make the capability understandable enough to use and predictable enough to rely on.

## Portfolio Review Framing

The portfolio repo is the source of truth for case-study content. Use this only as a presentation structure, and use `recruitment/portfolio-positioning.md` to choose which cases to emphasize.

### Recommended Flow

1. Open with the current focus: AI design leadership, marketplace/search systems, cross-platform product design, product systems, and senior IC judgment.
2. Show the strongest evidence for shipped product impact.
3. Show the strongest evidence for seniority, ambiguity, systems thinking, or AI leadership.
4. Add one supporting example only if it strengthens role fit.
5. Close by connecting the examples to the company's product problems.

### Case Framing Pattern

For each case, cover:

- Problem: what was broken, unclear, inefficient, risky, or strategically important.
- Stakes: why it mattered for users, the business, or the product system.
- Role: what Eddie personally owned, led, influenced, or decided.
- Constraints: what made the problem hard.
- Key decisions: 2 to 4 important product/design decisions.
- Tradeoffs: what was intentionally not done, simplified, delayed, or compromised.
- Outcome: public-safe, evidence-backed outcomes only.
- Relevance: what the case proves about Eddie's judgment for the target role.

### Seniority Without Process Theatre

Show seniority through problem framing, prioritization, tradeoffs, stakeholder alignment, product judgment, evidence discipline, systems thinking, cross-platform implications, and connecting strategy to delivery.

Avoid long process diagrams, generic double-diamond narration, inflated strategy claims, unsupported "I led" language, screenshots without decision context, and internal artifacts that cannot be explained publicly.

### Confidentiality

Use public-safe framing. Describe evidence types without showing confidential source material, generalize outcomes when exact metrics are not approved for public use, and avoid internal dashboards, unreleased roadmap details, stakeholder documents, experiment names, benchmarks, and sensitive screenshots.

## Questions For Recruiters And Hiring Managers

Use 3 to 5 questions per conversation.

### Role Scope

- What are the most important problems this person would own in the first 6 to 12 months?
- Is this role expected to define product direction, execute on an existing roadmap, or both?
- Where does this role sit: embedded in one product area, across multiple teams, or as a horizontal design lead?
- What would make someone successful at Staff / Principal level here?

### Team Maturity

- How mature is the design function today, and where does it need to level up?
- What are the biggest gaps in the current product/design process?
- How do design, product, engineering, and research typically work together?

### Success Criteria

- What would a strong first 90 days look like?
- What would success look like after one year?
- How will the role be evaluated: shipped product impact, team influence, quality of thinking, design craft, business outcomes, or something else?
- What would cause someone to struggle in this role?

### Design And Product Decision-Making

- How are major product decisions made today?
- Where does design have the most influence, and where is the team still trying to improve that influence?
- How often are designers involved before a solution direction is already chosen?
- How does the team balance speed, craft, and evidence?

### Hands-On Versus Leadership Expectations

- How much of the role is expected to be hands-on product design versus direction-setting and alignment?
- Does the team need someone to raise craft quality, shape strategy, mentor others, or directly drive a product area?
- Is this role more of a principal IC role, a design lead role, or a step toward management?
- How much time would this person spend working in Figma / prototypes versus meetings and strategy?

### AI / Product Strategy

- Where is AI core to the product today, and where is it still exploratory?
- What are the hardest AI product design questions the team is facing?
- How does the team think about trust, control, user feedback, and failure states in AI experiences?
- Is the design challenge more about inventing new AI-native workflows or integrating AI into an existing product system?

### Location And Work Style

- How important is location for this role?
- Is hybrid presence required for product collaboration, team culture, or company policy?
- If relocation is expected, what timeline does the company usually support?
- How would you describe the pace and operating style of the team?
- How does the company handle urgency without burning people out?

## Compensation And Location Language

Keep exact numbers and negotiation strategy in `recruitment/private/`.

### Broad Compensation Expectation

I am evaluating roles based on scope, company trajectory, product fit, and total package. For the right Staff / Principal IC role, I would want compensation to reflect the level of responsibility, market expectations, and the impact expected from the role.

### If Asked Early

I would prefer to understand the scope and level calibration first, because compensation should match whether this is truly Staff / Principal scope. Broadly, I am looking for a competitive total package for a senior IC product design role, including base, bonus if applicable, and equity where relevant.

### Relocation Tradeoff

I am open to relocation for the right opportunity, but I would weigh it against the role's scope, company trajectory, product fit, and total package. A relocation-heavy move needs to be clearly stronger than staying in Berlin.

## Tone Rules

Use a tone that is concise, confident, specific, human, senior without sounding inflated, direct about fit, and evidence-aware.

Avoid:

- "I am passionate about AI" language.
- Vague "driving innovation" language.
- Exaggerated ownership claims.
- Unsupported impact claims.
- Long autobiographical setup.
- Apologetic wording after rejection.
- Over-personal details in recruiter communication.

Good patterns:

- "This role stood out because..."
- "My background is strongest in..."
- "The relevant overlap is..."
- "I would be glad to discuss whether the scope matches what your team needs."
